I perceived it as the player spawn point. To make it feel safer and more inviting, I deliberately enhanced the brightness of the main light to fill the room with warmth, and added the floodlight to cast an orange tint on the right-side wall.

As the player step out of the spawn point, they would instantly noticed the presence of a flashlight. The flashlight targets at a warning sign, and informs the player what to check first.

This spot was intially too dark to distinguish, therefore I added a red warning light to the panel, to catch players' attention and invite them to check what's in the dark. It also cast a sharp contrast with the scene's overall teal tint.

As for the end of the corridor, it looks pretty plain and dull initially. I chose a lamp as a proper light source that stands right above the shelf, as it has a considerable beam angle.

This is a overview of how player would perceive this corridor, it's clear that the light casted onto the wall has successfully enhanced the overall contrast.

Looking for another light sources that could be seaminglessly integrated into the scene, I resorted to some LED light panels.

Without those panels, the corridor would undoubtedly look lack of detail as it's extremely hard to distinguish what's in those dark areas.

As for the stairway to the second floor, I deliberately placed a lightbulb to brighten up this area. Such brightness is well distinguished from the dark red ambient color.

It catches the players' attention, and may appeal to them as a hint that "there's more to explore if you cross the wire fence"

The opposite side of the corridor is fainted with dark red, as there's nothing significant. However, I sets a brighter purple tint in between, appealing to the player to explore the loots in the room.

I perceived here as the exit of this level, to adapt to its symmetric design, I set the color to contrast with each other, thereby reveals that the two exit might lead to difference consequences, and players themselves would have to make a choice.

I leveraged Lumen for fast iteration. Thanks to its dynamic global illumination, I can instantly tell a light would influcene the whole scene. Then I turned off Lumen and refered to the Lumen screenshots for creating the baked lighting.

In the Lumen workflow, a room is often lightened with one light source and the bouncing indirect lighting would then take over the whole room, whereas the traditional light baking workflow requires more light sources to correctly brighten up a room. As you could tell from below, I placed three more area lights to fake the bounce lighting.
